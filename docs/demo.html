01 THE QUESTION

What could you discover with infinite compute?

Discovery is a search over programs. Levin's one number — $Kt(p)=|p|+\log_2 t(p)$ — governs it and reads two ways (left); both are tractable shadows of the ideal predictor (right).

① COST  ·  compute to FIND p
$$2^{\,Kt(p)} \;=\; 2^{\,|p|}\,t(p)$$
Big = expensive. A longer or slower program takes exponentially more search. This is our x-axis: programs evaluated, $B$.
② WEIGHT  ·  how much we BELIEVE p
$$2^{-Kt(p)} \;=\; \dfrac{2^{-|p|}}{t(p)}$$
Big = believed. The reciprocal of cost — shorter ⇒ more probable. The minus sign lives here, in the belief.
weight = 1 / cost  ·  same $Kt$, flipped sign — the bill vs the belief
The ideal we approximate
$$M(x)=\!\!\sum_{p\,:\,U(p)=x}\!\!2^{-|p|}$$
Solomonoff prior — every program that fits, voting $2^{-|p|}$. Sum of all the weights on the left.
$$\mathbb{E}_M\!\left[x_{n+1}\mid x_{1:n}\right]=\dfrac{M(x_{1:n}\,x_{n+1})}{M(x_{1:n})}$$
Solomonoff's perfect expectation — the provably optimal prediction of the next observation. No computable predictor beats it. The gold standard.
$$K(x)=\min_{p\,:\,U(p)=x}|p|$$
Kolmogorov complexity — the single shortest program. What the weight points to as time → 0.
$$Kt(x)=\min_{p}\big(|p|+\log_2 t(p)\big)$$
Levin complexity — computable: length + compute. The two cards on the left are this, exponentiated.
$x$ the data $p$ a program = one hypothesis $U$ universal machine (our Python) $U(p)=x$ p reproduces the data $|p|$ length in bits ≈ AST nodes $t(p)$ runtime · capped 500 ms $M(x)$ Solomonoff prior $K(x)$ shortest $|p|$ $Kt(x)$ $|p|+\log_2 t$ $B$ programs evaluated
$$2^{-Kt(p)}\;\xrightarrow[\;\log_2 t(p)\,\to\,0\;]{}\;2^{-|p|} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad M(x)=\!\!\sum_{p\,:\,U(p)=x}\!\!2^{-|p|}$$
Free compute kills the $\log_2 t$ term: each program's belief collapses to pure simplicity $2^{-|p|}$; sum over all of them and you recover Solomonoff's $M(x)$ — the perfect expectation above. Meanwhile the cost is what a good prior buys down: DISCOVERY_PRICE = cost(anon) / cost(priors) — 12× on Rocket, 20× on Newton.

Appendix · mathematical foundations & Q&A armor

What we build on — and our lens

FunSearch (DeepMind, Nature 2023) and LLM-SR (ICLR 2025) established the method we use: an LLM proposes candidate programs, an evolutionary loop scores them against data, and the survivors seed the next round — proven, exactly-verifiable equation discovery. In-Context Symbolic Regression (Merler, 2024) pairs the LLM's proposed form with an external optimizer for the constants — our curve_fit step. We claim none of this engine as novel. Our contribution is a new lens on it: hold the engine fixed, switch the model's semantic prior on and off as the single independent variable, and read the compute gap that opens — the price of the prior. The same proven machinery, pointed at a question it was never used to ask.

Solomonoff induction — the identity of PRIOR_PRICE

Solomonoff induction predicts the next observation by summing over every computable program that reproduces the data, weighting each by $2^{-|p|}$ — Occam's razor as a probability measure.

$$M(x)=\!\!\sum_{p:\,U(p)=x*}\!\! 2^{-|p|}\qquad K(x)=\!\!\min_{p:\,U(p)=x}\!\!|p|\qquad P(p\mid D)\propto P(D\mid p)\,2^{-|p|}$$

It is uncomputable. Bound each program's runtime and you get Levin search — exactly what our 500 ms sandbox timeout enforces:

$$Kt(x)=\min_{p:\,U(p)=x}\big(|p|+\log_2 t(p)\big)$$

With infinite compute the $\log_2 t$ term is free, and the search converges to ranking by length alone — the Solomonoff-optimal hypothesis. That is the precise sense in which "infinite compute → ideal discovery."

Claim discipline. We compute an argmax, not the full Bayesian mixture; our program space is the LLM-reachable subset, not all Turing machines; $\ell(p)$ is a proxy for $K(p)$. We say "a tractable, LLM-guided MAP shadow of Solomonoff," never "we implement Solomonoff induction."

The objection — "isn't this just recitation?"

Kepler and Newton are in the model's training data, so semantic names may trigger recitation, not reasoning. Correct — and it is the experiment's boundary condition, not a flaw. Three reframes:

1 — we price substitutability, not discovery

Anything a prior provides, search (compute) can compensate, at a price of N× compute. Prior and compute are interchangeable currencies; the gap is the exchange rate. When compute is free, the premium vanishes.

2 — known laws are a calibratable proxy

You cannot measure a prior's worth on an unsolved problem — no ground truth. Known laws are the only place to calibrate it: the prior exists and the rediscovery is checkable. Calibrate on the known, extrapolate to the frontier, where the prior is weaker and the compute bill larger.

3 — the prior is a search shortcut, not the source of correctness

curve_fit on held-out data is the final arbiter; the prior only changes how fast the right form is proposed, never whether it is accepted. So the gap is pure search-shortcut value — a structure that holds on unsolved problems too.

Rehearsed answer. "Yes — they're in the training set, which is exactly why we can calibrate here: we need a prior that exists and a ground truth to check. We measure the compute to compensate a missing prior — 20× on Newton this run — which in the Solomonoff frame is the search bits a semantic prior saves over a uniform one. At the frontier the prior is weaker and the compute larger; when compute is free, a missing prior stops being a barrier. We put the first price on the prior↔compute exchange rate."

Why the measurement is clean · and what actually varies

  • Bitwise-identical data across conditions (only names + hint differ), enforced by test_auditability.
  • Honest x-axis: programs evaluated, never wall-clock. First-crossing of E_TARGET, excluding Occam refinement.
  • The gap is run- and law-specific. Newton this run: geomean 20.1×, per-seed 1.7× / 48.8× / 100.4× (heavy-tailed); arithmetic mean 50 ± 40×. Kepler: ~1.05×. A different theorem gives a different price. We report geomean + range, never a single point.
  • What is invariant is the limit $\Delta t=(B_{\text{anon}}-B_{\text{priors}})/R\to 0$ — true for any price.

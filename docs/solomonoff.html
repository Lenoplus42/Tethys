The Price of a Prior
A Solomonoff View of the Compute ↔ Truth Exchange Rate

Our engine is a resource-bounded, LLM-guided approximation of Solomonoff induction. This page makes that precise and folds in our measured runs: on Kepler the prior is worthless (~1×, our control); on Newton it is worth ~20× inference compute — and the engine rediscovers $a\,b/c^{2}$ blind. As inference throughput grows toward infinity, that price's felt cost falls to zero.

priors = learned prior ON anon = stripped → universal prior gap = PRIOR_PRICE Newton ≈ 20.1× · Kepler ≈ 1.05×

1 · Solomonoff induction in one screen

Solomonoff induction is the formally optimal way to predict the next observation from data. Its three load-bearing ideas are exactly the three our engine is built on: hypotheses are programs, the prior is a simplicity (length) prior, and the posterior keeps only programs consistent with the data.

$$\textbf{Universal prior:}\qquad M(x)\;=\!\!\sum_{p\,:\,U(p)=x*}\!\! 2^{-|p|} \qquad\qquad \textbf{Kolmogorov complexity:}\quad K(x)=\!\!\min_{p\,:\,U(p)=x}\!\! |p|$$

$U$ is a universal machine; $|p|$ is a program's length in bits. A hypothesis that reproduces the data with a shorter program is exponentially more probable — Occam's razor as a probability measure. Prediction is the Bayesian mixture over all consistent programs:

$$M(x_{n+1}\mid x_{1:n})=\frac{M(x_{1:n}\,x_{n+1})}{M(x_{1:n})}, \qquad\qquad P(p\mid D)\;\propto\; \underbrace{P(D\mid p)}_{\text{fit}}\;\cdot\;\underbrace{2^{-|p|}}_{\text{Occam prior}}.$$

Solomonoff induction is uncomputable: it sums over infinitely many programs and must know whether each halts. Every practical system — ours included — is a tractable shadow of it. The honest question is which shadow.

The computable cousin: Levin search

Bound each program's runtime and the theory becomes computable. Levin complexity penalizes length and computation time — which is precisely what our sandbox's hard timeout enforces:

$$Kt(x)\;=\;\min_{p\,:\,U(p)=x}\Big(\,|p|\;+\;\log_2 t(p)\,\Big)$$

So when a judge says "Solomonoff is uncomputable," the precise reply is: we run a Levin-style, time-bounded search, with the LLM as the search order. Our 500 ms per-candidate kill is the $\log_2 t(p)$ term made real.

Our selection rule (combine_score, lexicographic) is a MAP estimate under a Solomonoff-style length prior — we take the single best program rather than the full Bayesian mixture:

$$\hat p\;=\;\arg\max_{p}\;\Big[\,\mathbb{1}\!\left[\text{err}(p)<\epsilon\right]\,\big(C-\ell(p)\big)\;-\; \mathbb{1}\!\left[\text{err}(p)\ge\epsilon\right]\,\text{err}(p)\,\Big]\;\approx\; \arg\max_{p}\,\log P(D\mid p)\;-\;\lambda\,\ell(p).$$

Claim discipline. We compute an argmax, not Solomonoff's mixture; our program space is the LLM-reachable subset, not all Turing machines; and $\ell(p)$ (AST nodes) is a proxy for $K(p)$, which is itself uncomputable. We say "a tractable, LLM-guided MAP shadow of Solomonoff," never "we implement Solomonoff induction."

3 · Two priors, and the gap between them

Pure Solomonoff has one prior — the universal, domain-blind length prior. Our system stacks two: the universal/Occam prior (the length term) and the LLM's learned prior (which programs get proposed first). The LLM is a learned proposal distribution over program space — a smart importance sampler that replaces blind enumeration.

The ablation runs the identical engine with the learned prior switched on (priors) and off (anon) on bitwise-identical data. The horizontal distance between the two error-vs-compute curves is the headline number:

$$\text{PRIOR\_PRICE}\;=\;\frac{B_{\text{anon}}}{B_{\text{priors}}}\qquad\text{where}\qquad B_{\text{cond}}=\min\{\,B:\ \text{err}_{\text{cond}}(B)\le E^{*}\,\}.$$

In words: the compute cost of replacing a good learned prior with the universal one. Our control proves the metric is honest — a useless prior (Kepler) yields a ~1× gap; only a useful prior (Newton) opens a real one. The method does not manufacture gaps.

Why the measurement is clean — not a lucky number
  • Physically clean ablation. The two conditions' numeric data is bitwise-identical; only variable names + hint differ, enforced by a machine test (test_auditability). The gap is 100% attributable to the prior — not data or wording.
  • Honest x-axis. Compute is measured in programs evaluated (hardware-independent, reproducible), never wall-clock.
  • Honest crossing. PRIOR_PRICE is read at the first point past $E_\text{TARGET}$ — it excludes the post-convergence Occam-refinement rounds.
  • Heavy-tail-safe headline. Geometric mean, not arithmetic — the principled aggregate for a ratio, not dragged by one heavy seed.
  • Offline-reproducible. Every sweep saves a timestamped dir of complete RunLog JSON; the demo replays saved data with zero live-LLM dependency.

4 · The model — see the gap form

Both conditions approach the same irreducible error floor $E_\infty$ (noise / numerical precision); only the rate differs. A clean one-parameter model captures it:

$$\text{err}(B)=E_\infty+(E_0-E_\infty)\,e^{-B/\tau_{\text{cond}}} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; B_{\text{cond}}=\tau_{\text{cond}}\,\ln\!\frac{E_0-E_\infty}{E^{*}-E_\infty} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \boxed{\,\text{PRIOR\_PRICE}=\dfrac{\tau_{\text{anon}}}{\tau_{\text{priors}}}\,}$$

Because the data is identical ($E_0,E_\infty$ shared), the price collapses to a clean ratio of time constants. Defaults below are anchored to our measured Newton geomean (≈20×); drag to explore:

Honest findings

  • The control works. Kepler's prior is useless (c0*x**c1 is trivially discoverable blind), so the gap is ~1×. The metric distinguishes a useful prior from a useless one.
  • Robustly large on Newton. Every converged seed shows 1.7–100×, never ≈1.
  • Heavy-tailed / high-variance — intrinsic to a stochastic search over a hard form + non-deterministic LLM sampling. Report geomean + range, not a point.
  • Budget sensitivity. Newton-anon convergence: 1/3 seeds @ 300 → 2/3 @ 600 → 3/3 @ 800; priors always converges in round 1.
  • Program price ≠ token price. Tokens run higher (Newton 27× vs 20×) — the no-prior search burns more LLM rounds. We do not claim they agree.

6 · Near-infinite compute → infinitely close to Solomonoff

Two limits matter, and they differ. (a) As the compute budget grows, the best program our search has found converges to the shortest program consistent with the data — the Kolmogorov-minimal, Solomonoff-optimal hypothesis. The error rides down to its floor:

$$\lim_{B\to\infty}\text{err}(B)=E_\infty,\qquad\qquad \hat p_B \xrightarrow[B\to\infty]{}\ \arg\min_{p:\,\text{err}(p)\le E_\infty}\ell(p)\;\approx\;p_{K}\ \text{(the minimal program).}$$

(b) The PRIOR_PRICE ratio $B_{\text{anon}}/B_{\text{priors}}$ is scale-free — it does not vanish with more compute. What vanishes is the wall-clock felt cost. With throughput $R$ programs/sec, the time to reach the target is $t_{\text{cond}}=B_{\text{cond}}/R$, so the time you pay for the missing prior is

$$\Delta t \;=\; \frac{B_{\text{anon}}-B_{\text{priors}}}{R}\;\xrightarrow[R\to\infty]{}\;0.$$

That is the whole thesis in one limit: the prior's price is denominated in compute, and near-infinite inference throughput drives its felt cost to zero — the world Etched is building. 7 · The objection, answered — "isn't this just recitation?"

The sharpest challenge, and the one a careful judge will raise: Kepler and Newton are already in the model's training data. When it sees mass, distance, force and instantly proposes gravitation, it isn't reasoning from a prior — it's reciting the answer. So on a truly unsolved problem the prior wouldn't exist, and your 20× just measures how well the model memorized.

This is correct — and it is the boundary condition of the experiment, not a flaw. Stated openly, it becomes evidence of depth. Three reframes turn it into the pitch:

Reframe 1 — we price substitutability, not discovery

Our claim is not "the prior discovers new science." It is: anything a prior provides, search (compute) can compensate — at a price of N× compute. Prior and compute are two interchangeable currencies for buying discovery; the 20× is the exchange rate. On a new problem with no prior, you expect to pay a comparable compute premium — and when compute is free (Etched), that premium goes to zero.

Reframe 2 — known laws are a calibratable proxy

You cannot directly measure a prior's worth on an unsolved problem — by definition there is no ground truth to verify against. Known laws are the only place the quantity can be calibrated, because there both the prior exists (semantic names activate the model's knowledge) and ground truth exists (the rediscovery is checkable). We calibrate on the known, then extrapolate: at the frontier the prior is weaker, so the compute bill is larger — exactly why cheap compute makes frontier discovery feasible.

Reframe 3 — the prior is a search shortcut, not the source of correctness

In our pipeline curve_fit on held-out data is the final arbiter of correctness — the prior only affects how fast the right form is proposed, never whether it is accepted. So the 20× is pure search-shortcut value: the prior narrows the space, the data still judges. That structure holds on unsolved problems too — there the prior is weaker and the compute larger, which is the whole "cheaper compute → more frontier discovery" thesis.

Rehearsed answer. "Yes — Kepler and Newton are in the training set, and that is exactly why we calibrate here: we need a prior that exists and a ground truth to check. We don't measure 'the prior discovers new science'; we measure the compute it takes to compensate a missing prior — 20× on Newton. In the Solomonoff frame, that is the search bits a semantic prior saves over a uniform one. At the frontier the prior is weaker and the compute larger — and when compute is free, a missing prior stops being a barrier. We put the first price on the prior↔compute exchange rate."

The one-liner this page defends: "We run a Levin-style, time-bounded, LLM-guided MAP search — a tractable shadow of Solomonoff induction. The LLM's learned prior is our experimental knob; the gap between priors-on and priors-off measures, in inference compute, the price of that prior. Kepler (useless prior) is our ~1× control; Newton's prior is worth ~20×, and we rediscover $a\,b/c^{2}$ blind. As compute approaches infinite, that price's felt cost approaches zero. Known laws are where we calibrate the prior↔compute exchange rate; the frontier is where you spend it."
